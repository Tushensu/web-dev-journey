Chickens are one of the most common and widespread Domesticated animals , with a total population of 23.7 billion as of 2018, [3] up from more than 19 billion in 2011.[4] There are more chickens in the world than any other bird.[4]
There are numerous cultural references to chickens – in myth, folklore and religion, and in language and literature.

Genetic studies have pointed to multiple maternal origins in South Asia, Southeast Asia, and East Asia,[5] but the clade found in the Americas, Europe, t he Middle East and Africa originated from the Indian subcontinent. From ancient India, the chicken spread to Lydia in western Asia Minor, and to Greece by the 5th century BCE.[6] Fowl have been known in Egypt since the mid-15th century BCE, with the "bird that gives birth every day" having come from the land between Syria and Shinar, Babylonia, according to the annals of Thutmose III.

Terminology

An adult male is a called a cock or rooster (in the United States) and an adult female is called a hen.[10][11] Other terms are:

  • capon: castrated or neutered male chicken[a]
  • chick: young chicken[12]
  • chook /tʃʊk/: chicken (Australia, informal)[13]
  • cockerel: young male chicken less than a year old[14]
  • pullet: young female chicken less than a year old.[15] In the poultry industry, a pullet is a sexually immature chicken less than 22 weeks of age.[16]
  • yardbird: chicken (southern United States, dialectal)[17]

Historically, a chicken was any young domesticated bird, and the species was called domestic fowl or fowl.[18] Chicken may also mean a chick (see for example Hen and Chicken Islands).
